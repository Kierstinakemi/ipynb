Report

Abstract

The “volatilome” of human scent is closely correlated to the skin microbiome. It has been stated that human sweat has no odor until the skin microbiota begin to break down non-volatile compounds into volatile organic compounds (VOC) that are characteristic of human ‘scent’. For example, common microflora such as Corynebacterium spp, Bacillus spp and others are responsible for transforming long-chain fatty acids into short and medium chained products that can be further metabolized and volatilized--volatiles that are often associated with malodor. Skin microbiota, including fungi and viruses, occupy dynamic but unique niches within and on the human body. This ‘uniqueness’ can be exploited and applied to forensic identification and has been shown previously from touched samples. With the current advances in sequencing technology and “multi-tagged barcoding”, it is now possible to simultaneously sequence multiple samples and multiple taxa and/or genes all at the same time. Thus, a multi-taxon, multi-gene, targeted amplicon approach can be applied to characterize the microbiota profiles of an individual that would corroborate and correlate with human VOC profiles. Understanding this previous knowledge our study used techniques such as headspace SPME-GC/MS VOC analyses in conjunction with Length Heterogeneity PCR (LH-PCR) to screen the diversity of microbial community prior to conducting a Next Generation DNA sequencing run, to draft an optimized protocol to establish a correlation between microbial DNA (bacteria) and Human VOC profiles from the palms of hands.
